Millets are small-seeded grasses that have been cultivated for thousands of years across Asia and Africa. These hardy, drought-resistant grains were once a staple in Indian diets but gradually gave way to rice and wheat. Today, as we rediscover their incredible nutritional profile, millets are making a powerful comeback.

Types of Millets

Ragi (Finger Millet)

Rich in calcium and iron, perfect for bone health and anemia prevention. Ideal for children and women.

Bajra (Pearl Millet)

High in protein and fiber, excellent for heart health and diabetes management.

Jowar (Sorghum)

Rich in antioxidants and fiber, supports digestive health and weight management.

Foxtail Millet

High in iron and calcium, helps maintain healthy blood and strong bones.

Kodo Millet

Excellent for diabetes control with very low glycemic index and high fiber content.

Barnyard Millet

Highest fiber content among millets, great for weight loss and digestive health.

Little Millet

Rich in B-vitamins and minerals, supports metabolism and overall wellness.

Nutrient Facts

Protein: Millets contain 7-12% protein, comparable to wheat but with better amino acid profile.

Fiber: Millets have 2-3 times more fiber than refined wheat, promoting better digestion.

Minerals: Millets are significantly richer in iron, calcium, and magnesium compared to rice and wheat.

Vitamins: Excellent source of B-complex vitamins, especially niacin and folic acid.

Antioxidants: Higher antioxidant content than most common grains, supporting immune function.

Ragi: GI ~55-60 (Low)

Bajra: GI ~55-60 (Low)

Jowar: GI ~60-65 (Low-Medium)

Foxtail: GI ~50-55 (Low)

Kodo: GI ~50-55 (Low)

Barnyard: GI ~50-55 (Low)

Little: GI ~55-60 (Low)

For comparison, white rice has GI ~70-80 and wheat roti ~65-70.

For adults, 50-100g of millet flour per day is recommended as part of a balanced diet. This can be consumed as rotis, dosas, porridge, or other traditional preparations. Start with smaller portions if you're new to millets and gradually increase.
